Planting is effectively wrapped. The whole game now is fungicide and the first post-emerge passes. Corn is marching toward tassel and the wheat flag-leaf window is open in the South. This is the stretch a drone earns its keep, so book your fungicide acres before every grower in the county calls the same week.

$THE GOING RATE
 

The number to know this week is the one that beats the airplane.

$16/acre
University of Missouri's custom-hire drone rate (a crop duster runs about $12.50). On sticker price the plane wins, so you don't sell cheaper. You sell access.

Here is where you win the work. Tall corn at tassel is the classic case: a ground rig tears the canopy, and an airplane cannot push enough carrier volume into the upper canopy to do the job right. The drone flies low, slow, and targeted. Add wet fields that lock a ground rig out for days, odd-shaped fields a plane will not bother with, and buffer zones near houses and water, and the drone is not the cheap option, it is the only option. Price it on access, not on beating $12.50.

 
 
§THE FINE PRINT
 

Part 108 is the biggest rule change since Part 107, and it is aimed right at you. The FAA's final rule is expected to land any week now, so here is what an operator needs to know.

Part 108 creates a real framework for Beyond Visual Line of Sight flight, so you would no longer need a one-off waiver for every BVLOS job. It raises the BVLOS weight ceiling to 110 pounds, which pulls most loaded spray drones into scope for the first time. And it shifts responsibility from the individual pilot to the operator as a business, with new roles like Operations Supervisor and Flight Coordinator.

The catch ag operators flagged in their comments: the proposed rule limits drones from broadcasting ADS-B Out, which keeps you invisible to crop dusters and other low-altitude manned aircraft you share the air with. That is a safety fight worth watching.

WHAT TO DO NOW

Read the final rule when it lands, figure out whether your operation fits the permit tier or the certificate tier, and do not assume your current Part 137 setup covers BVLOS. It does not.

★THE TAILGATE
 

The ag spray drone is officially an export now. Agri Spray Drones, the Boonville, Missouri shop, expanded into Canada this spring and made its first Canadian hire up in Airdrie, Alberta. Picture the same drones that spray corn outside Boonville heading for canola country north of Calgary. Five years ago the spray drone was a novelty nobody trusted. Now it crosses borders. The business grew up fast.
