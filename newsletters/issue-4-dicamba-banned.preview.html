FROM THE CAB

Morning. Big one this week. There's a chunk of soybean herbicide work you legally cannot fly anymore, and a chunk of wheat fungicide work that just landed in your lap because the ground rigs are stuck in the mud. We'll sort out which is which. Plus fresh data on what your nozzles are actually costing you, and an update on the DJI fight that won't make you happy. Let's go.

 
 
WTHE WINDOW
 

Corn crossed the halfway mark, hitting 57 percent planted the week ending May 10, two points behind last year but five ahead of the average. Soybeans reached 49 percent, running ahead on both counts. Winter wheat slipped again to 28 percent good to excellent, the Southern Plains still hurting.

Tennessee and Kentucky are nearly done at 92 and 87 percent. Iowa hit 72, Illinois 54. The story for you this week is wheat. The flag-leaf to heading window is the single highest-payback fungicide pass on the wheat calendar, and it's open right now across a lot of the country. The widespread rains that slowed planting are the same rains keeping self-propelled sprayers parked. When a farmer can't get a ground rig into a soft field, the only thing that hits that flag leaf on time is a drone or an airplane. That's your week. Go book it.

$THE GOING RATE
 

Wheat fungicide is the paying work this week, so here are the numbers to quote.

$16/acre
University of Missouri's custom drone rate for a wheat flag-leaf pass (a crop duster runs about $12.50). All in with chemical, a farmer is looking at roughly $20 to $32.

Here's why he pays it instead of waiting. A self-propelled ground rig costs him about $2.50 to $3.50 an acre to run, cheaper on paper, but only if it can get in the field. Right now it can't. Soft ground means ruts, compaction, and a calendar slipping past the flag-leaf window while the disease keeps moving. The line to use: "Your ground rig is cheaper the day the field is dry. It's also parked. I can hit your flag leaf tomorrow." Sell the timing, because timing is the whole game on a fungicide.

 
 
TTANK TALK
 

New numbers worth your attention. Beck's Practical Farm Research ran corn fungicide trials in Lewiston, Illinois, comparing atomizers against flat-fan nozzles under heavy Southern rust pressure. The atomizers edged it, 267.7 bushels an acre versus 265. Small gap, but here's the part that matters: either setup beat the no-fungicide strip by 21 to 24 bushels an acre. That's an ROI in the neighborhood of $89 to $100 an acre on a single pass.

One more finding for your tank. Beck's tested a nitrogen solution as the spray carrier instead of plain water, and it lifted fungicide performance more on the drone than on the ground rig, a four-bushel swing in one field. Translation for your operation: your nozzle choice and your carrier are not afterthoughts. The drone rewards getting them right. If you're still running a setup straight out of the box, you're leaving bushels, and dollars, in the field.

 
 
§THE FINE PRINT
 

Read this before you load a soybean job. On February 6, the EPA registered three over-the-top dicamba products for the 2026 and 2027 seasons. The new labels carry the strictest rules the chemistry has ever had, and one of them is a flat aerial application ban that names unmanned aircraft directly.

BANNED FROM AERIAL APPLICATION
  • Stryax (Bayer, formerly XtendiMax)
  • Engenia (BASF)
  • Tavium (Syngenta)

You cannot put any of these three over the top with your drone. Period.

These are Restricted Use Pesticides, so only a certified applicator can touch them, and a certified applicator who sprays one of them aerially is the one FIFRA comes after. The agency was blunt: violations bring civil fines and, for knowing ones, criminal prosecution. What to do this week: learn the three names cold, and when a bean farmer asks you to fly his dicamba, the answer is no. Then redirect him. Plenty of other herbicides and fungicides are still labeled for UAS. Take that work and leave the federal case for somebody else.

 
 
CTHE CHINA WATCH
 

The DJI door isn't reopening any time soon. The FCC's comment window closed May 11, and the day before the deadline the tension peaked: Commissioner Olivia Trusty stood up at an AI and security expo on May 7 and defended the Covered List in plain terms, casting drones as the next security battleground and asking "who will write the rules of a connected and intelligent airspace." That's not the sound of an agency about to back down.

On the other side, the Drone Service Providers Alliance, which represents more than 33,000 working pilots including ag service companies, filed a sharp comment arguing the FCC can't have it both ways. Either these drones are too dangerous to fly at all, in which case ground the fleets already working public safety, infrastructure, and farms, or the risk depends on how they're used, in which case a blanket ban is overkill. It's the strongest argument operators have. It's also up against a commissioner who just told a room she's not wavering. Read the room: keep babying your DJI, price your next bird, and take a hard look at the four US-made systems that got conditional approval through the end of 2026.

★THE TAILGATE
 

Down in Georgia they're flying drones over the champagne of onions. Vidalia season is in full swing, official pack date was April 13, and the festival drew crowds and an air show the last weekend of April. If you don't know the rules on these: you legally cannot call an onion a Vidalia unless it's grown in a specific patch of southeast Georgia dirt, and the state will fine you up to $1,000 for selling a fake. So naturally somebody's now putting a spray drone over the most regulated onion in America. There's a video. It's exactly as wholesome as it sounds, a Georgia onion farmer and a flying spray tank, and a nice reminder that this isn't just a Corn Belt game.
