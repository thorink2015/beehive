FROM THE CAB

Morning, and happy short week. Planting is just about wrapped, which means the season has fully tipped over into fungicide work, the back half that pays your year. This issue is built to win you that work: hard numbers that bury the old "drones cost too much" objection, the EPA blueprint that's about to reshape every fungicide label, and an Autel court fight worth watching. Let's go.

 
WTHE WINDOW
 

The Crop Progress report came a day late this week thanks to Memorial Day, but it landed Tuesday with corn at 86 percent planted as of May 24, three points ahead of the five-year average. Soybeans hit 79 percent. Iowa was 94 percent done, Illinois 86. Winter wheat slipped one more notch to 26 percent good to excellent, and the first Southern Plains wheat is starting to come off.

Planting is effectively over for most of the country, and the forecast for this week is friendly, drier where it's been wet and rain where it's been dry. That's your green light. The whole game now is fungicide timing. Corn marches toward tassel and the wheat flag-leaf and early-heading window is live. This is the stretch where a drone earns its keep, so get your fungicide acres booked before every farmer in the county is calling the same week.

$THE GOING RATE
 

Time to bury the oldest objection in the book: "the drone costs more." It doesn't, and now there's math to prove it.

$31 vs $30/acre
Drone versus ground rig, all in (Nelson Drone Solutions, Nebraska corn and beans). The drone cuts chemical use ~40 percent, so the bottom line lands in a dead heat.

The application line looks higher on the drone, but the chemical savings close the gap to a single dollar. Then the yield question, which used to be the comeback. Beck's Hybrids ran multi-year plots, and 2025 was the first year with heavy disease pressure, exactly when a low-volume drone was supposed to fall apart. Corn with no fungicide made 227 bushels. The ground rig made 235. The drone made 237, on one-eighth the water. It matched or beat the ground rig in the hardest year to do it.

And here's the cost nobody puts on the invoice: the days the ground rig can't run. Wet spring, standing water in the low ground, corn too tall for the clearance frame. The drone flies in every one of those. If a fungicide window is 7 to 10 days and rain eats 4 of them, the drone is the difference between getting it on and missing it entirely. Same money, same yield, and it shows up when the ground rig is parked. That's the whole pitch. Memorize it.

 
§THE FINE PRINT
 

EPA dropped the draft Fungicide Strategy on May 1, and you should care because fungicide is your paycheck for the next two months. It is not law yet. It's the blueprint for how fungicide labels will tighten to protect endangered species, the third piece after the Herbicide and Insecticide Strategies you've already met. Expect the same machinery you saw on Liberty ULTRA: mitigation points and Bulletins Live! Two checks baked into future labels. It covers roughly 41 million treated acres a year across the lower 48.

One detail in your favor: the draft proposes accepting more spray-drift adjuvants, including guar gum, which is good news for low-drift drone application.

COMMENT BY JUNE 29

Read EPA's draft Fungicide Strategy and file under docket EPA-HQ-OPP-2026-2973. The rules getting written right now are the ones you'll spray under for the next ten years. Better to have a say than to find out later.

 
CTHE CHINA WATCH
 

Autel came out swinging. On May 11, the company filed a reply with the FCC in Docket 26-23, arguing it was added to the Covered List based on classified evidence it was never allowed to see, and on technical complaints that were actually aimed at DJI, not Autel. It's a Fifth Amendment due-process case, leaning on a 2014 court ruling that said even national-security decisions can't rest on secret, category-wide assumptions without showing a company the unclassified record against it.

Autel also did something new. It put sworn declarations on the public record stating that its flight data is stored locally by default and is not automatically uploaded to company servers. That's the data-sovereignty point a lot of operators have wanted on paper. Nothing changes today: Autel is still on the list and still blocked from new authorizations. But if you fly an Autel, the company is fighting, and "my flight data stays on my controller" is now a line you can give a nervous landowner with a citation behind it.

 
“HEARD AT THE HEADLAND
 

Jnani Matson, who runs Vortex Advantage AG out of Alvarado, Texas, on where the work actually comes from:

A lot of the business I got was from landowners over the age of 85.
Jnani Matson, Vortex Advantage AG

Sit with that one. The customer isn't always the big row-crop operator with a planter and a drone of his own. It's the older landowner who can't get on a rig anymore and has rough, brushy, or wet ground that still needs treating. That's a whole market the $8 volume crowd isn't even looking at.

Source: Farm Equipment

 
AOPEN ACRES
 

Planting's done and you're staring down a full fungicide season, which is exactly the right moment to line up winter. The work does not have to stop in November. Solar-farm vegetation, utility right-of-way, cover-crop seeding, mosquito control, brush on rangeland, it all runs in the cold months, and most of it gets contracted in summer, not December. Start those conversations now while you've got cash flow and a fresh season of references to point at. The operators who make it through their first winter are the ones who booked it in July, not the ones who went looking in October.

 
★THE TAILGATE
 

The "ag company first" outfit just left the country. Agri Spray Drones, the Boonville, Missouri shop run by Taylor Moreland, expanded into Canada this spring and hired its first Canadian employee, an ag sales specialist up in Airdrie, Alberta. Picture it: the same drones spraying corn outside Boonville are now headed for canola country north of Calgary. Five years ago the ag spray drone was a novelty nobody trusted. Now it's a US export. The business grew up fast.
