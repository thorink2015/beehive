## The Window

Planting came in hot. Corn hit 11 percent in the ground the week ending April 19, ahead of the 9 percent five-year average, and soybeans ran to 12 percent against an average of just 5. The South is way out front. Tennessee was already 64 percent planted on corn against a 24 percent norm, and Kentucky 48 percent.

Wheat country got the short straw. Winter wheat sat at 30 percent good to excellent, with the Southern Plains baked dry.

**What it means for you this week:** burndown and pre-plant is the paying work right now, and the early start means your spray calendar just got shorter. If a farmer is dragging his feet on booking, that's your opening. Get the acres committed before the post-emerge rush stacks up.

## The Going Rate

For the first time, there's a real university number to point at.

:::stat $12.50/acre
2026 Iowa State Custom Rate Survey average. $12.00 median across 47 operators, low end at $8. Down 30 to 45 percent from where rates sat in 2022.
:::

Here's the line to use on a farmer who says he'll just buy his own rig: University of Missouri pegs custom hire around $16 an acre, and owning a drone only beats that if he's spraying close to 1,000 acres a year. Below that, you're cheaper than the loan payment. Say it just like that.

## The Fine Print

The BVLOS rule, Part 108, is expected to land as a final rule this spring. Before anyone panics: Part 107 and Part 137 are not going anywhere. Part 108 is aimed at autonomous, beyond-line-of-sight flying, mostly bigger aircraft, with the company on the hook for training instead of the individual pilot.

The ag aviation crowd has been loud about it, warning of a visibility gap and collision risk down low where you and the spray planes both work. Worth watching. Not worth losing sleep over yet. If you fly within sight of your bird, nothing changes for you this season.

## The China Watch

If you fly a DJI Agras, read this twice. After the December 23 deadline for a national security review passed, the FCC added all new foreign-made drones to its Covered List. The good news: nobody can ground or shut off a drone you already own. Keep flying it.

The catch is the next purchase. New imports and authorizations are blocked, and even a small change to a covered drone after the listing could trigger a new approval, which may gum up firmware updates. Protect the fleet you've got, and start thinking hard about what your next bird is.

## The Tailgate

MSU ran its Spring Spray Drone Fly-In up in Benton Harbor on April 8, and the part everyone showed up for was the side-by-side showcase: different makes and models flown next to each other in the same field. Basically a drone drag race with spray tanks.

If your dealer ever tells you his bird covers more ground than the other guy's, this is the cure. Fly them in the same dirt and watch the swath. Numbers on a sheet lie. A field doesn't.
