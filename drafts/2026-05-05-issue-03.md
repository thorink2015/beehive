:::intro From the cab
Morning. The big number this week is $13 an acre, and if that's what you're quoting on commodity row crops, you're racing eleven other guys to the bottom. Good news: the money didn't disappear. It moved. We'll show you where. Plus a Monday deadline at the FCC you'll want to hit, and an FAA program that just ended the era of polite warnings. Let's go.
:::

## The Window

Corn jumped to 38 percent planted the week ending May 3, ahead of the five-year average. Soybeans hit 33 percent, running ahead of both last year and the average. Winter wheat ticked up to 31 percent good to excellent, which sounds like progress until you remember the Southern Plains is still parched.

Weekend frosts and freezes hit the Northern Plains and may have set some fields back, and a wide rain front is moving across the country this week. Read that as a tight, stop-start spray window for most of you. The South is rolling into post-emerge and the first scouting passes. The Corn Belt finally got a planting stretch, so if the rain holds off where you are, the early-season weed work is yours to grab. Watch the wind around the front. The calm hours are going to be short.

## By the Numbers

:::numbers
$13/acre | the 2025 national average for drone spraying, per the American Spray Drone Coalition. Down 38 percent from $21 the year before.
16.4 million | acres treated by drone in 2025, up 58.7 percent in a single year.
Down 59% | new drone unit sales over the same stretch. More acres, fewer new machines. The fleets already out there are flying harder than ever.
$5/acre | what some operators say they're clearing in profit at current commodity rates. That's the squeeze, in one number.
:::

## The Going Rate

So rates cratered to $13 and the bottom feeders are working for $5 profit. Here's the move: stop selling the spray everybody else can do, and start selling the spray only a drone can.

Specialty crops haven't collapsed. Orchards, vineyards, and vegetables still run $18 to $40 an acre because the work is harder and the competition is thinner. And in tall corn at tassel, the drone is often the only tool that fits: a ground rig tears up the canopy and an airplane can't put down enough carrier volume up top.

:::stat $27.26/acre
Beck's Practical Farm Research return on drone-applied fungicide in corn. That's the number to wave at a farmer, not the $13.
:::

The line to use: "I'm not the cheapest guy with a drone. I'm the guy who can spray the field nobody else can reach without wrecking your yield." Sell the wheel tracks you save and the canopy you don't crush. Quote the value, not the floor.

## The Fine Print

The FAA just ended "we'll let you off with a warning." On April 17 a new program called DETER took effect, and it changes the math on a slip-up.

Here's the plain version. For years a first-time minor violation got you a warning and a talking-to. Now most of those go straight to formal enforcement. DETER offers a fast-track settlement, a reduced penalty or a shorter suspension, but only if you're a first-time offender, only on a small drone under 55 pounds, and only if you admit liability and give up your right to appeal, with 10 days to settle.

Catch for our crowd: your loaded T40 or T50 is not a small drone. Anything 55 pounds and up at takeoff doesn't get the fast-track at all. A violation on the big bird goes through full enforcement, no discount. On top of that, local police can now flag the FAA in real time.

:::note
**What to do Monday.** The World Cup runs June 12 to July 19 and will throw temporary flight restrictions all over the map. Keep your Remote ID broadcasting, check for TFRs before every job, and don't be the cautionary tale your buddies forward around.
:::

## The China Watch

DJI put a dollar figure on the squeeze. In an April 15 filing to the Ninth Circuit, the company said the FCC's Covered List has voided 14 existing product authorizations and blocked 25 planned 2026 launches, 39 products in all, for a projected $1.56 billion hit this year. One of the casualties is the Mavic 3 Multispectral, the scouting bird a lot of you fly, with no US-made equal at that price.

:::note
**Deadline Monday, May 11.** The FCC's public comment window on the DJI and Autel petitions closes end of day. If you fly DJI and want a say in whether the door reopens, file a comment in ET Docket 26-22 at the FCC's ECFS site. Spell out the actual operational harm: the bird you can't replace, the job you lost. "I like DJI" gets ignored. "I can't buy the replacement my insurer requires" gets read.
:::

## Open Acres

Market signal worth filing away. The Texas Farm Bureau warned back in January that limited drone and parts availability could keep farmers from managing their spray programs. Read that again from the operator's side. When farmers worry the supply of machines is thin, the operator who already owns the fleet and shows up reliable becomes the safe bet. That's your repeat business. Lock in the farmers who can't, or won't, buy their own rig, and do it before the next guy does.

## The Tailgate

If your idea of a rough field is a slightly wet headland, go watch a West Virginian spray a cliff. Out in Doddridge County, the operators with Kuhn's Aerial Applications are putting drones on pasture so steep a tractor would roll. Pilot Nicholas Kuhn says about 90 percent of his work is brush, autumn olive, multiflora rose, ironweed, goldenrod, on slopes where the only other option is a guy with a backpack sprayer and a prayer.

Down near Huntington, Derrick Jackson at Jackson Aerial Ag flies the same kind of ground and put it best: "If you take care of the land, the land is going to take care of you." Flatland Corn Belt operators, this is your humility check. There's a whole market in the terrain you've never had to think about.
