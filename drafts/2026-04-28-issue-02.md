## The Window

Corn hit 25 percent planted nationally the week ending April 26, three points ahead of last year and six ahead of the five-year average. Soybeans ran to 23 percent. Winter wheat held at 30 percent good to excellent, no change from the week before, and the Southern Plains is still dry.

The South is basically done with the early work. Tennessee was 80 percent planted on corn, Texas 71, Kentucky 69. If that's your country, burndown and pre-plant pay is drying up and your next paying acres are post-emerge and the first fungicide scouting passes. Get those farmers on the calendar now.

The Corn Belt is the opposite story. Iowa sat at 22 percent corn and 11 percent soybeans, both behind last year. If you're up there, you've still got a full burndown and pre-plant window in front of you. Don't let a wet week make you think the season passed you by. It didn't.

## The Going Rate

:::stat $16/acre
University of Missouri's custom-hire benchmark. The number to put in front of a farmer who's still on the fence.
:::

Here's the math that closes him, straight from Russell Hedrick, the North Carolina farmer who holds the world dryland corn yield record. Take 6,000 acres of soybeans. Run a ground rig through them just twice and lose a buck and a half a bushel to wheel-track damage. That's $90,000 gone. Now spend $51,000 on a drone setup that covers 750 acres a day. Eight days to cover the whole farm, $90,000 in damage saved, and you're still $39,000 ahead. The drone paid for itself in eight days.

You don't have to sell the drone. Sell the wheel tracks he's already paying for. That's the pitch.

## The Fine Print

Check your inbox. If you're sitting on a Part 137 exemption petition, the FAA may have sent you a Request for Information, and it is not optional.

The agency found exemption holders running ag operations outside the limits of their exemptions, so it started operational validations. Translation: they're emailing operators to confirm the basics, your Remote Pilot Certificate, your drone registration, your tail number, your operating details. If you don't answer the RFI, the FAA closes your petition for failure to respond. No decision letter, no exemption, back to square one.

:::note
**What to do Monday.** Search your email and your spam folder for anything from the exemptions office. If it's there, answer it fully and answer it fast. Questions on the RFI itself go to 9-AVS-ARM320-Exemptions@faa.gov. Don't let a dead exemption be the thing that grounds you in the middle of fungicide season.
:::

## The China Watch

If you fly a DJI Agras, the rules around your next purchase tightened again. Back on December 22, the FCC put all foreign-made drones on its Covered List, which blocks new equipment authorizations for DJI, Autel, and XAG. Say it one more time for the room: nobody can shut off the bird you already own. Keep flying it.

The squeeze is on replacement. New imports and authorizations are blocked, and the American-made alternatives from Hylio and Ceres Air run three to five times the price of the Chinese hardware they'd replace. On top of that, combined tariffs are sitting near 35 percent, and the Section 122 surcharge that's piled on is set to expire July 24. Whether that relief actually lands is anybody's guess. The move this season: baby the fleet you've got and start pricing your next bird now, before the decision gets made for you.

## Open Acres

Market signal worth filing away. The Texas Farm Bureau warned back in January that limited drone and parts availability could keep farmers from managing their spray programs. Read that again from the operator's side. When farmers worry the supply of machines is thin, the operator who already owns the fleet and shows up reliable becomes the safe bet. That's your repeat business. Lock in the farmers who can't, or won't, buy their own rig, and do it before the next guy does.

## The Tailgate

Somebody stole 15 spray drones in New Jersey and the FBI lost sleep over it. On March 24, a guy posing as a delivery driver rolled up to a logistics company in Harrison with a forged bill of lading and a fake confirmation email, and the staff loaded 15 Ceres Air C31 units onto his truck. That's $58,000 a piece, about $870,000 driven off the lot on paperwork alone. The bureau called it one of the most sophisticated thefts they'd seen in years, mostly because a C31 can dump 31 gallons across 15 acres in eight minutes and they did not love thinking about who might want that.

The ending's almost a letdown. New Jersey State Police found all 15 in a warehouse in Dover on April 27, the day before you're reading this. No arrests yet. The drones never even left the state. Lesson for the rest of us: the most dangerous thing in ag aviation this month wasn't a crash. It was a clipboard and a confident handshake.
