## The Window

Corn hit 25 percent planted nationwide, soybeans 23, both running well ahead of the five-year averages of 19 and 12. Winter wheat is 34 percent headed, and Kansas is 43 percent headed, 32 points ahead of normal (USDA NASS, week ending April 26).

That headline matters by region:

- **Plains:** south-central Kansas wheat is past the optimal fungicide window. But flag-leaf-to-flowering fields in northwest and north-central Kansas are still candidates if leaf rust shows. No stripe rust confirmed yet, but leaf rust reports are already up out of Oklahoma.
- **Corn Belt:** ground rigs are sidelined on wet fields. Ohio jumped from 9 to 20 percent corn in a week. This is your compaction-free pitch. You spray where the sprayer would leave ruts.
- **Delta and South:** keep moving. Tennessee is 80 percent corn planted, Texas 71. The dry window is open and it won't last.

## The Going Rate

:::stat $16.00/acre
University of Missouri Extension G1274 custom-hire benchmark for drone spray application. Farmer-owned cost runs $12.27/acre at 1,000 acres a year, $7.39 at 4,000.
:::

Cross-reference it with the Iowa State 2026 survey average of $12.50 an acre, and Rantizo's Ben Etsinger says Indiana and points east will pay up to $20 while Iowa sits just under $15. Use the $16 as your anchor when a customer pushes back, and let geography explain the spread. Acres sprayed in the US went from 4 million to 10.3 million between 2023 and 2024, so supply is growing faster than demand. That's why rates compressed.

## The Fine Print

The FAA is mailing Part 137 exemption holders a Request for Information, and the line that should get your attention is theirs, not mine: without your response, "the FAA will not move forward with a decision letter and will close your petition request for failure to respond to the RFI."

If you get an email from an faa.gov exemptions address, answer it. Have this ready:

:::note
- Full legal name and a physical street address. A P.O. Box will not be accepted.
- Chief Supervisor of Operations name and contact.
- PIC name and Part 107 certificate number.
- Drone make, model, and N-number, plus your Part 137 certificate number.
- The name on your Form 8710-3 must exactly match the name on your exemption. "Bob Smith" and "Bob's Drone Ops LLC" are two different entities to the FAA.
:::

The regulator spent years lowering the bar to entry. This is the year it starts checking that the people who walked through understand the rules.

## The China Watch

Start with the number you feel in the wallet: Chinese-made spray drones carry roughly 35 percent in combined tariffs right now (25 percent Section 301 plus a 10 percent surcharge added February 24). That surcharge is set to expire July 24.

For context, the FCC added DJI and all foreign-made drones to its Covered List effective December 22. New models can't get US authorization, which blocks normal import and sale. Your T40, T50, or T100 already in the barn is still legal to fly. The risk is parts, firmware, and replacement, not airworthiness. US-built options from Hylio and Ceres Air exist but run three to five times the price of the Chinese hardware. If you're expanding in 2026 or 2027, budget for it and plan the lead time now.

## The Tailgate

Fifteen US-built Ceres Air spray drones, about $870,000 worth, walked out of a Harrison, New Jersey warehouse on March 24 on a forged shipping document. The FBI's Joint Terrorism Task Force worked it as a possible chemical-dispersal threat. A retired agent said the bureau was "freaked out for a good reason."

The punchline: the drones turned up a month later, untouched, sitting in plain sight at a storage facility in Dover, 30 miles away. No arrests. Each one carries a 31-gallon tank and sprays 15 acres in eight minutes. Every operator with a trailer in the barn just got a fresh reason to padlock the gate.
